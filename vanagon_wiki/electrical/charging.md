## Overview

The Vanagon uses a standard alternator charging system. The alternator
is driven by the same belt as the water pump and fan. A failed
alternator or voltage regulator leaves you stranded.

## Normal System Voltage

| Condition | Voltage |
|-----------|--------|
| Battery at rest | 12.4-12.7V |
| Engine running (charging) | 13.8-14.4V |
| Low voltage warning | Below 12V with engine running |

## Charge Indicator Light

**If the charge indicator light comes on while driving — STOP.**

This is not a "check soon" warning. It means the alternator has stopped
charging. The battery will be depleted within 20-30 minutes of driving,
leaving you with no ignition, no fuel pump, and a dead van.

GoWesty has a specific article: "Charge Indicator Light: This Means STOP!"

## Voltage Regulator

The voltage regulator is internal to the alternator on most Vanagon years.
A failing regulator causes:
- Overcharging (voltage above 14.8V) — boils the battery
- Undercharging (voltage below 13.5V) — battery slowly dies

## Belt Inspection

The alternator/water pump belt should be inspected at every service.
A broken belt simultaneously kills the alternator AND the water pump —
instant overheating and electrical failure. Replace if cracking is visible.

## Battery

Standard group 41 or 42 battery fits most Vanagons.
The battery is in the engine area on early models, or under the passenger
seat on some configurations — check your specific van.

## Westfalia Auxiliary Battery

Many Westfalia campers have a second "house" battery for the camper
appliances (refrigerator, lights). This is separate from the engine
battery. The two batteries are connected via an isolator relay that
prevents the house battery from draining the engine battery.

If the house battery is not charging: check the isolator relay and
the charging circuit to the house battery.
