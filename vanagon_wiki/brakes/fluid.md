## Specification

- **Type:** DOT 3 or DOT 4
- **DOT 4 preferred** — higher boiling point, better for mountain driving
- Change interval: **every 2 years** regardless of appearance

## Why Change Brake Fluid Regularly?

Brake fluid is hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture from the air over time.
Absorbed moisture lowers the boiling point significantly. In mountain
driving (Eastern Sierra, mountain passes), brakes generate tremendous heat.
Boiling brake fluid causes brake fade and potentially complete brake loss.

Fresh DOT 4 boils at 230°C / 446°F dry. Moisture-contaminated fluid
can boil as low as 140°C / 284°F — well within reach of normal use.

## Reservoir Location

The brake fluid reservoir is located under the instrument cluster in
the dashboard. To access:
1. Grasp the two recesses on the back of the frame cover
2. Pull up to access the reservoir
3. Check fluid level between MIN and MAX markings
4. Vehicle must be on level surface, engine off

**Low fluid warning:** If the fluid level is below MIN, check for leaks
before simply adding fluid. Low fluid usually indicates worn pads
(piston extended further) or a leak — not just evaporation.

## Bleeding Procedure

The Bentley recommends bleeding in this order: rear right, rear left,
front right, front left (furthest from master cylinder first).

Brake fluid strips paint — protect body panels during bleeding.
Use a proper brake bleeding kit or have a helper pump the pedal.

## Dark or Contaminated Fluid

If the fluid is dark brown or has a burnt smell, flush the entire
system. Contaminated fluid accelerates seal deterioration and can
cause caliper/wheel cylinder failure.
