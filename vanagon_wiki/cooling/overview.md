## How the WBX Cooling System Works

The 2.1L Wasserboxer uses a front-mounted radiator with coolant piped
the full length of the van to the rear-mounted engine. This long coolant
circuit is unique among production vehicles and creates specific
maintenance requirements.

### Key components:
1. **Front radiator** — mounted behind the front grille
2. **Long coolant pipes** — run under the van, approx 12 feet total
3. **Water pump** — engine-driven, circulates coolant when running
4. **Auxiliary water pump** — electric, runs after shutdown
5. **Thermostat** — controls coolant flow based on temperature
6. **Expansion tank** — front of van, pressurized system

## The Aux Pump — Why It Matters So Much

When you shut off a WBX engine, the water pump stops immediately.
But the cylinder heads retain heat and can boil coolant locally.
The auxiliary electric water pump continues circulating coolant for
several minutes after shutdown to prevent this.

**If the aux pump fails, the engine can suffer heat damage every single
time it is shut off after a hard run.** This damage accumulates invisibly
until the head gasket fails. See [[cooling/aux-water-pump]]

## 1986 Cooling System Redesign

VW completely redesigned the WBX cooling system for 1986. The new system:
- Had fewer components
- Was much better at keeping air out
- Was easier to maintain
- Prevented the chronic overheating issues of the 1983-1985 1.9L WBX

The 1986-1991 system is significantly more reliable than the early WBX
if properly maintained.

## Coolant Specification

- **Type:** G11 (blue/green) or compatible ethylene glycol
- **Mix:** 50/50 antifreeze/distilled water
- **Capacity:** Approx 10 quarts total system
- **Change interval:** Every 2 years regardless of mileage

**Note:** Do not mix G11 (blue/green) with G12 (pink/red) coolants.
The WBX was designed for G11. Using distilled water (not tap water)
prevents mineral deposit buildup in the narrow cooling passages.

## Filling and Bleeding the System

Air trapped in the WBX cooling system causes overheating and is
notoriously difficult to bleed completely. The "Libby Bong" method
(a pressurized fill technique using a bottle) is a well-known community
solution. TheSamba FAQ has detailed threads on this procedure.

Always fill slowly with engine warm and thermostat open.
