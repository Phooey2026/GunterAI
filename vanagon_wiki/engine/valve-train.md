## Overview

The WBX uses hydraulic lifters (no manual valve adjustment required) and
pushrods enclosed in tubes that pass through the cooling fins. The pushrod
tube o-rings are a common source of oil leaks on high-mileage engines.

## Hydraulic Lifters

### Cold start noise
Ticking or clattering on cold start that disappears when warm is normal
for high-mileage WBX engines. Oil drains from the lifters when the engine
sits. If noise persists when warm, investigate further.

### Persistent lifter noise
- Check oil level first
- Check oil pressure — see [[engine/oil-system]]
- Consider an oil flush if sludge buildup is suspected
- Collapsed lifters may require replacement

### Bleeding lifters
If lifters are noisy after an oil change or after the engine has sat for
a long time, they may need to be bled. Procedure involves running the
engine at moderate RPM to work oil into the lifters.

## Pushrod Tubes

### Common leak points
The pushrod tubes seal at both ends with o-rings. These o-rings harden
and crack with age and heat cycling. Oil leaks from the pushrod tube ends
are very common on high-mileage WBX engines.

### Inspection
With the engine running, look for oil seeping from where the pushrod tubes
meet the cylinder heads and the crankcase. Any leak here will worsen over time.

### GoWesty collapsible pushrod tubes
GoWesty makes collapsible pushrod tubes that can be installed without
removing the engine. Standard pushrod tube replacement requires engine removal.
The collapsible tubes are a popular upgrade worth doing during any major engine work.

### O-ring replacement
If doing a head gasket job or engine-out service, always replace all pushrod
tube o-rings. They are cheap insurance against future leaks.
